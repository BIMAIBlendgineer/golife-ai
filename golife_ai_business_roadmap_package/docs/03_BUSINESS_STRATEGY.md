# 03 - Business Strategy

## Market hypothesis

People do not need another task app, another pantry app, or another budget app.
They need one layer that reduces daily friction by turning scattered inputs into a few realistic actions.

GoLife AI should enter the market as:

> the app that turns your day into 3 useful missions.

Not as:

- a superapp;
- a generic productivity tool;
- a finance app;
- a meal-planning app;
- a wardrobe app.

## Initial segments

### Segment 1 - Overloaded people

Traits:

- too many pending tasks;
- weak weekly structure;
- poor habit continuity;
- frequent overwhelm.

Message:

> "Your day, turned into small missions."

### Segment 2 - Friction-sensitive savers

Traits:

- repeated micro-spending;
- impulse purchases;
- low financial clarity;
- dislike of spreadsheets.

Message:

> "Catch small money leaks before they get expensive."

### Segment 3 - Anti-waste households

Traits:

- buy without a clear plan;
- forget what is already at home;
- throw away edible food.

Message:

> "Buy less, use more, waste less."

### Segment 4 - Conscious consumers

Traits:

- repeated clothing purchases;
- weak awareness of what they already own;
- interest in reducing consumption.

Message:

> "Your closet already solves more than you think."

## Core differentiation

Most apps optimize one domain.
GoLife AI should coordinate decisions across domains only when that changes what the user should do today.

Example:

```text
Regular planner:
  "You have 8 tasks today."

GoLife AI:
  "8 tasks are not realistic today. Finish 2 critical tasks,
   use food that expires tomorrow, avoid a new purchase,
   and protect 10 minutes for your minimum habit."
```

## Entry wedge

The launch wedge must be:

**AI Daily Missions**

A single surface that turns:

- tasks;
- habits;
- basic expenses;
- available pantry items;

into 3 to 5 missions for today.

Everything else exists only if it improves those missions.

## Sellable MVP

Included:

- quick capture;
- daily missions;
- explanation and uncertainty;
- usefulness feedback;
- lightweight weekly planning;
- basic expense reflection;
- basic pantry rescue.

Excluded from MVP:

- social/community;
- banking integrations;
- mandatory OCR;
- marketplace;
- family collaboration;
- wearables;
- complex wardrobe visuals;
- full calendar integrations.

## Competitive strategy

Do not compete head-on with Todoist, YNAB, pantry apps, wardrobe apps or Notion as separate products.
Compete on synthesis:

> one AI that helps you make better small decisions across real life.

## B2C vs B2B

### B2C first

Simple, faster to validate, App Store friendly.

### B2B later

Possible only after the consumer loop is proven and privacy controls are much stricter.

## Main strategic risk

The product becomes too broad before it becomes useful.

Every release must answer:

> Does this improve today's decision quality?

If not, the feature should be rejected or moved out of the MVP.
